Experiment Proposal: Nonlocal Parity Gate for Semi-Quantum Key Agreement

Explanation:
We consider two “classical” users, Alice and Bob, who can only prepare and send single photons in the Z basis (|0⟩ or |1⟩). A third party, Charlie, is fully quantum-capable and holds an ancilla Bell pair. The goal is to implement, in a single linear-optical network, the nonlocal parity function f(x,y)=x⊕y between Alice’s and Bob’s input bits. Charlie’s post-selected measurement both heralds success and directly yields the key bit. Because Alice and Bob only ever prepare Z-basis states and never perform any active operations, this realizes a semi-quantum key agreement primitive: 
  – Each round, Alice sends |x⟩, Bob sends |y⟩.  
  – The network entangles these with Charlie’s Bell pair via beam splitters.  
  – Charlie performs entanglement-swapping measurements on ancilla modes.  
  – Successful coincidence patterns herald and output one photon whose presence in detector “K1” (vs. “K0”) encodes x⊕y.  
  – Charlie announces “success,” and Alice and Bob learn the shared key bit x⊕y.  

Visualization (modes from top to bottom):
  [Alice emitter]───┐
                   ├─▷  linear-optical network ──▷ detectors {A_out,B_out,K0,K1}
  [Bob   emitter]───┘
  [Bell-pair source]───┐
                       ├─▷ (same network)
  [Bell-pair source]───┘

Node roles:
  - Inputs 0,1: Alice and Bob’s single photons (classical bit in Z basis).
  - Modes 2,3: Charlie’s Bell-pair ancillas (probabilistic pair source).
  - Network: beam splitters and phase shifters connecting all four modes.
  - Detectors:
      • A_out, B_out: unmonitored (they simply carry through Alice/Bob’s qubits and are discarded).
      • K0, K1: number-resolving detectors; a click in K1 heralds parity =1, click in K0 heralds parity =0.  

Target Quantum Behavior:
Post-selection on a single-click event in {K0,K1} implements:
  |x⟩₀ |y⟩₁ |Φ⁺⟩₂₃  →  |junk⟩ A,B  ⊗ |0⟩K0|1⟩K1 with amplitude δ_{x⊕y,1}
                          or |1⟩K0|0⟩K1 with amplitude δ_{x⊕y,0}
for x,y∈{0,1}. In other words, the detection mode “parity” carries exactly x⊕y, and all other photons are routed to unobserved outputs.

Why It’s Interesting and Generalizable:
  - Fundamental demonstration of a nonlocal XOR gate via entanglement swapping.  
  - Direct primitive for semi-quantum key agreement (SQKD + QKA): two classical parties jointly agree on a secret bit without quantum operations of their own.  
  - Easily extended to n-party parity (compute the XOR of n classical inputs) by chaining multiple Bell-pair ancillas and intermediate swap measurements.  
  - Opens up a new family of “distributed Boolean-function evaluation” experiments in linear optics, beyond standard teleportation/swapping, with applications in quantum networks and multiparty cryptography.
