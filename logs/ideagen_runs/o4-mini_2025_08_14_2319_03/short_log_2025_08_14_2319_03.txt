Title: Distributed high-sensitivity sensor via entanglement swapping with embedded mode-transform interferometers  

Explanation:  
We design a four-node linear-optical network that both distributes entanglement and amplifies an unknown phase (or misalignment) in a chosen spatial‐mode basis. Nodes A and D are the distant “sensors,” each holding one half of a Bell pair. Between them are two intermediate swapping stations, B and C, each of which performs (1) a Bell-state measurement on two incoming photons and (2) a fixed mode transformation—specifically, a d×d discrete Fourier transform (DFT) on spatial modes—implemented by a multiport interferometer.  

1. Sources:  
   – Two independent probabilistic photon-pair sources prepare Bell pairs between A–B and C–D.  

2. Node B:  
   – Receives photon from A’s pair and one from the A–B source.  
   – First applies a d-mode DFT interferometer (the “mode transformer”).  
   – Performs a Bell measurement on these two photons.  

3. Node C:  
   – Receives the other half of the A–B pair (already through the B transformer) and one photon from the C–D source.  
   – Applies the same d-mode DFT.  
   – Performs a second Bell measurement.  

4. Outcome:  
   – After heralding on the two Bell measurements, nodes A and D share a d-dimensional maximally entangled state—but now in the Fourier basis set by the two embedded DFTs.  
   – Small mismatches in the mode transformations (e.g. an unknown phase φ added in one arm before the DFT) become amplified in the entanglement correlations and can be estimated with Heisenberg‐like sensitivity because the entangled state spans d modes with a collective phase dependence.
